01 — About the Project

Why hand movement, and why now

Clinical-grade motion capture is expensive, bulky, and confined to specialist labs. NeuroSense explores whether a coin-sized inertial sensor and open-source signal processing can bring objective hand-movement measurement within reach of far more research settings.

The Problem

Movement assessment today relies heavily on subjective visual observation or six-figure motion-capture rigs — leaving a wide gap for accessible, quantitative tools.

Objectives

Build a wearable that streams accelerometer and gyroscope data, extract meaningful motion features, and present them clearly — without any diagnostic claims.

Research Motivation

Early academic literature suggests inertial measurement units can capture tremor and coordination signals comparable in shape to laboratory-grade sensors, at a fraction of the cost.

Technology Used

04 — Research

Paper & documentation

Abstract

We present NeuroSense, a low-cost wearable system for quantitative assessment of hand-movement characteristics using inertial sensing. By combining an ESP32 microcontroller with an MPU6050 six-axis sensor, the system streams motion data to a Python-based pipeline for filtering and feature extraction. This work does not constitute a diagnostic tool; it establishes an accessible measurement instrument for research and screening-support contexts.

Objectives

Design a wearable sensor unit under $30 in component cost, achieve a stable sampling rate suitable for tremor-band frequencies, and produce a reproducible feature set describing amplitude, frequency, and stability of hand motion.

Methodology

Participants perform a standardized set of hand-motion tasks while wearing the sensor unit. Raw signals are filtered, segmented, and passed through a feature-extraction pipeline producing the metrics shown in the dashboard.

Expected Outcomes

A validated, low-cost data-collection instrument and an open feature-extraction pipeline that other research groups can adapt for their own motion studies.
