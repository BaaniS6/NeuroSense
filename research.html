01 — Project Overview

What NeuroSense is — and isn't

NeuroSense is a low-cost wearable platform for measuring hand-movement characteristics using inertial sensors and open-source signal processing. It is built as an educational and research tool for exploring accessible motion-instrumentation methods — it is not a medical diagnostic device, and it does not identify, diagnose, or treat any condition. Its purpose is to make quantitative movement data easier to collect and study outside specialist laboratories.

Objective

Design and validate a sub-$30 wearable sensor unit capable of capturing repeatable, research-grade hand-motion signals for study and screening-support contexts.

Innovation

Pairing a commodity IMU with an open signal-processing pipeline and a web dashboard lowers both the cost and the technical barrier to quantitative motion research.

Future Vision

A reproducible, open-hardware measurement platform that other student and academic research groups can adopt, extend, and validate independently.

02 — Research Questions

What we're trying to find out

Four questions frame the current phase of the project and guide what gets measured, built, and tested next.

Q1

Can low-cost IMUs quantify hand movement?

Whether a consumer-grade accelerometer and gyroscope can capture motion features with enough consistency to be scientifically useful.

Q2

Can wearable sensors objectively measure movement stability?

Whether stability and variance-based metrics derived from raw signals correlate with observable movement characteristics.

Q3

Can web dashboards improve interpretation of motion data?

Whether a browser-based visualization layer helps researchers read and compare sessions faster than raw data logs.

Q4

How can affordable biomedical technology support research?

Whether lowering hardware cost meaningfully expands who can run motion-measurement studies, and where.

Kinematics and rigid-body motion underpin how raw acceleration and rotation readings map onto real hand movement. Key concepts: linear acceleration, angular velocity, reference frames.

Human Movement

Biomechanics

Understanding hand and wrist range of motion informs sensor placement and task design. Key concepts: joint articulation, tremor bands, postural control.

Electronics

Embedded Systems

Electronics

Microcontroller I/O, serial communication, and power management make the wearable practical to build and run. Key concepts: I2C, sampling rate, power budget.

Signal Processing

Filtering & Features

Signal Processing

Turning noisy raw signals into stable, comparable metrics. Key concepts: low-pass filtering, FFT, feature extraction.

Web Technologies

Visualization

Web Technologies

Browser-based dashboards make session review accessible without specialist software. Key concepts: data visualization, client-side charting, responsive UI.
